# Style Guide — Piotrek's Voice

Distilled from exemplars in `deep-dives/style/`. This is the standing reference the critique loop reads every round. When the user adds or changes exemplars, re-distill this file — raw posts are the source, this is the compiled artifact.

## The target voice

Paul Graham's essays plus observation-driven tech-Twitter. A person reasoning out loud, landing on a claim, willing to be wrong in public. Two moves define it:

1. **Argument from a sharp premise.** PG opens with a claim that sounds almost obvious, then turns it. "If the key to becoming a billionaire is to exploit people, as some politicians claim, we at YC are idiots." The structure is: state the opposing view plainly, follow it to its absurd conclusion, let the reader feel the contradiction. Don't hedge the setup — commit to it, then break it.
2. **Personality through observation, not adjectives.** "I have a few guy friends in NYC who are single, and they no longer use any of the dating apps. That wasn't the case even a few years ago." A specific, true, small detail does the work an opinion can't. Notice the thing, then say what it might mean — lightly.

## Rhythm (the thing to check first)

Write like music, not a metronome. Short sentence. Then a longer one that carries the actual idea and has room to breathe. Then a fragment. Vary tempo constantly. One-word or one-line paragraphs are allowed and good. If three sentences in a row are the same length, break one.

Read it for the ear. If it sounds weird aloud, it's weird on the page.

## Hard rules — flag every violation

- **No "It's not X, it's Y."** This construction is a tell. "It's not a productivity tool, it's a thinking partner" → "It's a thinking partner." or "Productivity tool? Barely. It thinks with you."
- **No em dashes.** Use periods, commas, or parentheses. (This is the single strongest AI tell for this user — catch every one.)
- **Simple words only.** Small words, big ideas. use/help/show/prove/improve, not leverage/facilitate/underscore/showcase/optimize.
- **Banned vocabulary (AI-detector words):** delve, leverage, showcase, underscore, navigate, harness, transformative, landscape, seamless, robust, adept, crucial, comprehensive, absolutely. Plus corporate choreography: unlock value, drive synergies, accelerate innovation, empower stakeholders, embark on a journey. If a word sounds like a keynote deck, flag it.
- **Nouns and verbs over adjectives.** "The platform cut onboarding from three weeks to three days," not "the incredibly innovative, transformative platform."
- **No warm-up, no recap.** Cut the intro that announces what's coming and the conclusion that repeats the intro. Get in, say it, leave.

## Soft pulls — nudge toward these

- **Write closer to thought.** Jumps, fragments, a claim that arrives before its justification. People don't think in tidy paragraphs.
- **Leave gaps.** Don't explain everything. A little inference is what makes a reader lean in. Trust them.
- **Surprise.** Change angle, use an unexpected example, introduce a little tension. Predictability is death.
- **A little roughness is the point.** Perfectly sanded prose reads synthetic. Clarity first, polish a distant second. Never critique toward grammatical perfection — for this user, slight roughness is a feature.

## What NOT to flag

Grammar slips. Incomplete sentences. Abrupt transitions. Lowercase starts. These are the style, not defects. The only "correctness" that matters: claims are sourced or labeled as the user's guess, and jargon is translated.

## How to use exemplars in critique

Name 2–3 concrete deltas between the draft and the exemplars — rhythm, opening move, directness, how uncertainty is handled. Always concrete ("your three sentences are all medium-length; PG would cut one to four words"), never vague ("be more like PG"). Exemplars set direction, never a costume: if a change makes the draft sound more like PG but less like Piotrek, drop it. His voice wins.
